Present Simple vs Continuous Stative Verbs Person Adjectives Adverbs of Manner
Present Simple vs Present Continuous
Present SimplePresent Continuous
UseRoutines, facts, permanent situationsNow, today, this week, temporary/changing
Formverb (+s for he/she/it)am / is / are + verb-ing
Signalalways, usually, often, every daynow, at the moment, today, these days
ExampleI often wear a jacket.I'm wearing a jacket today.
"What do you do?"= What's your job?"What are you doing?" = right now
-ing Spelling Rules

Most verbs: + ing (wear → wearing) · End in -e: drop e + ing (live → living) · One vowel + one consonant: double (sit → sitting, run → running) · End in -y: just add ing (play → playing)

Stative Verbs — NOT used in continuous

like · love · hate · know · understand · want · need · believe · remember · seem

✓ I like chocolate.   ✗ I am liking chocolate.
Person Adjectives & Opposites
kind — mean · polite — rude · hard-working — lazy · generous — selfish · brave — cowardly · calm — nervous · honest, clever, funny, patient, helpful, shy, quiet, talkative, serious
Adjective vs Adverb

Adjective describes a noun; adverb describes a verb. Usually + ly.

AdjectiveAdverb
She is a careful driver.She drives carefully.
He is a good singer.He sings well. (good → well)
They are hard workers.They work hard. (no change)
She is a fast runner.She runs fast. (no change)

Watch out: hardly = almost not · lately = recently.

Whether / If

Used to introduce indirect questions or reported questions. Both whether and if have the same meaning here.

Sam was asking whether I have any time for the class.
I don't know if she is coming.
